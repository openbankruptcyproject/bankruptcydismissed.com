Bankruptcy Dismissed in Massachusetts

Understanding dismissal of bankruptcy cases in MA. Consequences, refiling rules under Section 109(g), and recovery paths.

What Dismissal Means

Dismissal ends a bankruptcy case without discharge. The debtor's debts remain owed in full. Any automatic stay protection ends. Creditors may resume collection actions immediately upon dismissal.

Dismissal is different from discharge denial. A dismissed case can often be refiled (subject to Section 109(g)). A case where discharge was denied cannot produce a discharge on the same debts in the same chapter.

Common Reasons for Dismissal

  • Failure to file required schedules, statements, or tax returns
  • Failure to appear at the 341 meeting of creditors
  • Failure to complete pre-filing credit counseling or post-filing debtor education
  • Failure to make Chapter 13 plan payments
  • Bad faith filing
  • Failure to provide requested documents to the trustee
  • Substantial abuse under Section 707(b) means test
  • Voluntary dismissal by the debtor

See complete list of dismissal grounds.

The 180-Day Bar - Section 109(g)

If a prior case was dismissed for failure to appear or obey court orders, or was voluntarily dismissed following a motion for relief from the automatic stay, Section 109(g) bars refiling for 180 days.

The 180-day bar applies when:

  • The prior case was dismissed for willful failure of the debtor to abide by court orders or appear before the court in the case
  • The debtor requested and obtained voluntary dismissal after a creditor filed a motion for relief from the automatic stay

Refiling After Dismissal

Most dismissals do not trigger the 180-day bar. In those cases, the debtor may refile immediately. However, a refiling after dismissal may have limited automatic stay protection:

  • Second filing within one year: stay lasts only 30 days unless extended by the court (Section 362(c)(3))
  • Third filing within one year: no automatic stay attaches unless the court orders otherwise (Section 362(c)(4))

See refiling mechanics.

Recovery Path in MA

If your case was dismissed, recovery options depend on why it was dismissed:

  • Dismissed for failure to file documents: Gather the missing documents and refile, or file a motion to reconsider
  • Dismissed for non-payment in Chapter 13: Evaluate whether Chapter 7 is now an option, or whether circumstances have changed
  • Dismissed for bad faith: Significant evidence of changed circumstances required for refiling
  • Voluntarily dismissed: Reevaluate the financial situation before refiling

If the case was dismissed while you had counsel and you believe the attorney's conduct contributed to dismissal, consider a fee review under Section 329(b). Region 1 U.S. Trustee oversees fee review in Massachusetts.
