Dismissed Bankruptcy on Your Credit Report

How dismissed vs discharged bankruptcies appear on credit reports and affect your score

How Long Does It Stay on Your Report?

A dismissed bankruptcy stays on your credit report for:

  • Chapter 7 dismissed: 10 years from the filing date
  • Chapter 13 dismissed: 7 years from the filing date

This is the same duration as a completed bankruptcy. The fact that the case was dismissed does not reduce the reporting period.

Dismissed vs Discharged: Credit Impact

From a credit scoring perspective, a dismissed bankruptcy is often worse than a discharged one:

  • Discharged: Debts are eliminated, your debt-to-income ratio improves, and you can start rebuilding immediately with a clean slate.
  • Dismissed: The bankruptcy notation appears on your report, but none of the debts were eliminated. Your debt-to-income ratio remains unchanged. You got the credit damage without the debt relief.

This is why financial advisors often say that a dismissed bankruptcy is the worst outcome -- you take the credit hit without getting the benefit.

Can You Remove It?

You generally cannot remove a dismissed bankruptcy from your credit report before the reporting period expires. However, you can dispute inaccurate information:

  • If the bankruptcy is reported as "discharged" when it was actually "dismissed," dispute the status with the credit bureau
  • If individual accounts still show as "included in bankruptcy" when they were not discharged, dispute those entries
  • If the filing date is incorrect (which affects when it falls off), dispute it

Rebuilding After Dismissal

Since none of your debts were discharged, your path to credit recovery involves addressing the underlying debts. Options include:

  • Refile bankruptcy: If you are eligible, a successful case that results in a discharge will actually help your credit by eliminating debt. See refiling guide.
  • Debt management plan: A credit counseling agency can negotiate lower interest rates and payments
  • Negotiate settlements: Many creditors will accept less than the full balance
  • Secured credit card: Begin building positive payment history even while dealing with existing debts
